Discover the World of Diamonds

Shapes, cuts, grading, sourcing and how to buy with confidence

What is a diamond?

Diamonds are a crystalline form of carbon created under high pressure and temperature deep inside the earth's mantle. They are valued for their optical properties — brilliance, fire and scintillation — and for their durability (the hardest natural material). A diamond’s desirability depends on many factors including cut, carat, color and clarity, often summarized as the 4Cs.

At Payal & Minal Jewellers we source, cut and certify diamonds to ensure ethical sourcing, accurate grading and long-term value for our customers.

Tip: A well-cut diamond often appears larger and brighter than a larger poorly cut stone. Prioritise cut first when choosing a diamond for maximum visual impact.

The 4Cs — Cut, Carat, Color, Clarity

Cut (the most important)

The cut determines how well a diamond’s facets interact with light. A precise cut increases brilliance and fire. Cut grades (as defined by GIA and other labs) typically range from Excellent/Ideal → Very Good → Good → Fair → Poor.

  • Proportions: Table size, crown angle, pavilion depth and girdle thickness influence light return and dispersion.
  • Symmetry & Polish: Symmetry refers to how evenly the facets are arranged; polish describes surface finish. Excellent symmetry & polish improve sparkle.
  • Visual impact: A slightly smaller but well-cut diamond will generally look more brilliant than a larger poorly-cut one.
Carat (weight)

Carat is the unit of weight for diamonds (1 carat = 200 mg). Carat affects price non-linearly: prices increase at popular weight thresholds (0.50, 0.75, 1.00 carat, etc.). Consider the following:

  • Face-up size vs weight: Two diamonds with the same carat can look different in diameter depending on cut depth and proportions.
  • Price steps: Slightly under popular weights (e.g., 0.95 ct vs 1.00 ct) can save significantly while appearing visually similar.
  • Budget strategy: Decide desired visual size first, then find the best cut and clarity within that size and budget.
Color

Diamond color grading normally follows a scale from D (colorless) to Z (light yellow/brown). Colourless diamonds (D–F) are rare and command a premium. Near-colorless (G–J) offer great value and can appear colorless once mounted.

  • Fancy colors: Strongly colored diamonds (pink, blue, yellow) are graded and priced differently; they are called fancy color diamonds.
  • Setting choice: A warm gold setting can mask slight body color; platinum or white gold emphasizes colorlessness.
Clarity

Clarity measures internal (inclusions) and external (blemishes) characteristics. Common clarity grades: FL (Flawless), IF (Internally Flawless), VVS1–VVS2, VS1–VS2, SI1–SI2, I1–I3.

  • Eye-clean: Many SI and even some I grade diamonds can be eye-clean (no visible inclusions to the naked eye). Choose based on whether the inclusion affects brilliance.
  • Location matters: Inclusions under the table or near the edge impact appearance differently. Clear, well-placed inclusions can keep prices lower without affecting beauty.
Which C to prioritise?Cut, then Carat (size preference), then Color, then Clarity — balancing all four within your budget gives the best visual and value outcome.
How they affect priceCut and Carat cause the steepest price differences; premium cuts and larger carat weights increase cost disproportionately compared to small changes in clarity or color.

Shapes & Popular Cuts

Shapes describe the outline and facet arrangement. The cut is the facet configuration used to produce optical performance. Below are common shapes and what they offer:

Round Brilliant

Most popular — optimized for maximum brilliance and fire with 57–58 facets. Ideal/Excellent cuts maximise light return.

Princess

Square silhouette with brilliant facet pattern; modern and sparkly. Offers excellent yield from rough stones.

Emerald

Step-cut with long facets; elegant, emphasizes clarity and color rather than brilliance.

Oval

Brilliant cut with elongated shape that can make the finger appear longer; watch for 'bow-tie' dark area.

Marquise

Elongated brilliant cut that maximizes perceived carat size; prone to chipping at points — consider protective setting.

Cushion & Radiant

Cushion has rounded corners and a vintage look; radiant blends emerald's outline with brilliant facets for sparkle.

Cutting, Polishing & Finishing — from rough to gem

The transformation from rough diamond to polished gem is a careful, multi-step process combining science and craft:

  1. Analysis & Planning: Experts (using tools like 3D mapping and scanning) determine optimal yield and cut to maximize value. Planning balances size vs clarity vs weight retention.
  2. Preforming / Sawing: The rough is sawn or laser-sliced into workable pieces; poor angles or rough inclusions are removed.
  3. Bruting / Shaping: The rough is shaped into the intended outline (especially important for round cuts).
  4. Faceting: The stone is faceted — crown, pavilion and table — to exact angles. Precision here affects light return directly.
  5. Polishing: Each facet is polished to optical smoothness. Polish grade (Excellent → Poor) is part of lab grading reports.
  6. Inspection & Certification: After cutting, diamonds are graded by independent labs (GIA, IGI, HRD, AGS, etc.) for the 4Cs and given a report or certificate.

Polish & Symmetry: Polish is about the surface finish; symmetry addresses the exactness of facet arrangement. Both affect the final appearance and are reported on certificates.

Grading, Certificates & Trusted Labs

Reputable diamonds come with a detailed grading report from an independent laboratory. Important labs include:

GIA (Gemological Institute of America)

Globally respected for rigorous, consistent grading. GIA reports provide Cut, Carat, Color, Clarity, polish, symmetry and a plotted diagram of inclusions.

IGI (International Gemological Institute)

Widely accepted; IGI reports include similar details to GIA and are common for finished jewellery inventory.

AGS, HRD & others

AGS (notable for cut grading), HRD (Antwerp) and other labs are also trusted; always compare grading criteria and ask for a report copy.

Tip: Always ask for a lab report copy and verify the report number on the issuing lab's online database when possible.

Treatments, Synthetic Diamonds & Detection

Not all diamonds are entirely natural or untreated. Common categories to be aware of:

  • Untreated natural diamonds: Mined and polished without clarity or color enhancements — generally preferred and valued.
  • Treated diamonds: Methods like laser drilling, fracture filling, high-temperature annealing or HPHT (High Pressure High Temperature) can improve appearance. Treated stones should be disclosed and priced accordingly.
  • Lab-grown (synthetic) diamonds: Chemically and optically similar to natural diamonds but created in laboratories. They are an ethical and cost-effective option; they must be disclosed and usually come with their own certificates.

Detection: Modern laboratories have tools (spectroscopy, inclusion analysis, UV/fluorescence, growth pattern analysis) to distinguish treated, synthetic and natural diamonds. Buy from dealers who provide clear disclosure and lab documentation.

How to choose a diamond — Buying guide

  1. Decide your budget & preferred visual size. Choose carat range and then prioritise the best cut within that budget.
  2. Prioritise cut and proportions. Choose the best cut grade you can afford — it most affects sparkle.
  3. Choose color based on setting. For white metal (platinum/white gold), aim for G–I/ D–F for premium colorlessness. For yellow gold, G–J+ can be excellent value.
  4. Choose clarity for eye-clean appearance. Look for SI1–VS grades that are eye-clean rather than paying for microscopic clarity where not necessary.
  5. Ask for a recent lab certificate. Verify serial/report number online; avoid sellers who can't provide documentation.
  6. Inspect in person when possible. Lighting and surroundings affect appearance; see the stone in similar lighting conditions to where it will be worn.
  7. Consider lifecycle factors. Insurance, servicing, resizing and trade-in policies—these affect long-term ownership.

Care, Cleaning & Insurance

Diamonds are hard but not indestructible. Follow these care tips:

  • Regularly clean with warm water, mild detergent and a soft brush to remove oils and dirt that reduce brilliance.
  • Avoid ultrasonic cleaners for some treated or fragile settings; check with your jeweller first.
  • Store separately to avoid scratches to other gemstones or metalwork; diamonds can scratch softer gems.
  • Insure high-value items; keep photographs and copies of certificates and invoices for claims.
  • Have mounts and prongs checked annually to ensure stones are secure.

Ethical Sourcing & The Kimberley Process

Responsible sourcing is essential. The Kimberley Process restricts trade in conflict diamonds, but credible dealers also follow supplier audits, chain-of-custody records and traceability practices. At Payal & Minal Jewellers, we:

  • Provide clear certification and disclosures for all natural diamonds.
