All about Gold

History, karats, colors, crafting methods, sourcing, testing and buying advice

Why gold matters

Gold has been treasured since ancient times for its beauty, scarcity and chemical stability. Beyond jewellery it has served as currency, an industrial metal and a store of value. For jewellery, gold offers excellent ductility, a warm lustre and the ability to alloy with other metals to create different colors and mechanical properties.

Quick facts: Gold (Au) is a chemical element (atomic number 79), extremely malleable and resistant to tarnish; 1 gram of gold can be beaten into a sheet of many square meters.

History & cultural importance

Gold’s use dates back thousands of years — from the ancient Egyptians' burial treasures to classical empires, through medieval coinage to modern investment and jewellery. Societies prized gold for rituals, adornment and as a visible sign of wealth and status. Different cultures developed their own techniques for working gold: granulation in ancient Etruria, filigree in South Asia, and sophisticated gem-setting in Europe.

Over centuries, gold also became central to global trade networks and monetary systems (the gold standard era). Today it remains both a luxury material and an industrial commodity used in electronics, medicine and aerospace.

Karat & purity — what the numbers mean

Gold in jewellery is almost never pure 24K (100% Au) because pure gold is very soft. Instead it is alloyed — mixed with other metals — to increase strength and create colors. The karat (K) system measures purity:

  • 24K = 99.9%+ pure gold — very soft, usually used in bullion not everyday jewellery.
  • 22K = 91.6% gold — common in South Asia for traditional jewellery; softer than lower karats but rich in colour.
  • 18K = 75% gold — popular for high-end jewellery (good balance of purity & durability).
  • 14K = 58.5% gold — very common in Western markets for durable jewellery.
  • 10K = 41.7% gold — minimum karat that can be legally called gold in some countries (US).
Karat vs Hallmark

Karat indicates gold content; a hallmark is an official stamp marking purity and sometimes the assay office. Look for recognized hallmarks and maker marks when buying jewellery.

Gold colors & alloys

Gold’s color is modified by alloying metals. Popular color varieties:

Yellow Gold

Classic warm tone created by mixing gold with copper and silver. Widely used in traditional jewellery and wedding bands.

White Gold

Alloyed with white metals (nickel, palladium, zinc) and usually plated with rhodium for bright white finish. Rhodium plating may wear off and require re-plating over time.

Rose / Pink Gold

Contains higher copper content, giving a warm pink hue; extremely popular in modern and vintage-inspired pieces.

Other color treatments include green gold (with silver & copper balance), grey golds (with palladium), and blackened finishes (surface treatments or alloys). Remember: color influences style, maintenance and price.

Where gold comes from — global supply

Major gold-producing countries include China, Australia, Russia, United States, Peru, Ghana and South Africa. Mining ranges from large industrial open-pit operations to artisanal and small-scale mining. Responsible sourcing is important: look for suppliers with transparent supply chains and certifications.

Recycled gold

A growing share of new jewellery uses recycled gold (refined from old jewellery and industrial scrap). Recycled gold reduces environmental impact since it avoids new mining. Many reputable jewellers now offer recycled-gold collections and track chain-of-custody.

Shaping & crafting gold — techniques

Goldsmithing combines traditional handcraft with modern technology. Important methods include:

Casting (Lost-wax)

A wax model is made, invested in plaster, burnt out and molten gold poured to produce the basic shape. Widely used for rings, pendants and complex shapes.

Forging & Fabrication

Sheet and wire are cut, formed, soldered and hammered to create bespoke pieces. Fabrication gives strong, hand-finished results favored in artisanal jewellery.

Granulation & Filigree

Ancient decorative techniques: granulation uses tiny gold beads; filigree uses twisted wires to form lace-like patterns, common in South Asia and Mediterranean jewellery.

Electroforming & Plating

Electroforming can create lightweight gold shells over models; plating (e.g., vermeil) coats a base metal with a layer of gold. Plated items are more affordable but plating thins over time.

Stone Setting & Finishing

Stone setting (prong, bezel, pavé) secures gems; final finishing includes polishing, rhodium plating (for white gold), brushing or texturing to achieve desired look.

Modern tools

CAD/CAM and 3D printing allow precise prototypes and efficient production, combined with traditional hand-finishing for quality pieces.

Testing, Hallmarking & Certification

Always confirm gold purity and authenticity. Common tests and marks:

  • Hallmarks: Government or assay office stamps indicating purity (e.g., 750 for 18K, 916 for 22K). Look for reputable assay marks or BIS hallmark (India), UK Assay Office marks, etc.
  • Acid test: Quick field test showing reaction on touchstone — useful but can be imprecise and may leave micro-marks.
  • XRF (X-ray fluorescence): Non-destructive analyzer that reports elemental composition — used by professionals and assay labs.
  • Fire assay: Highly accurate destructive test for bullion and large lots; used for refining and official purposes.
What to ask the jeweller

Request the hallmark, a written description of karat & weight, and any warranty/service terms. For large purchases insist on lab assay or invoice detailing purity and maker.

Market pricing & ratings

Gold price fluctuates daily on global markets (spot price). Jewellery pricing = metal value (based on daily gold rate × weight × purity) + making charges + gems + taxes. Ratings and quality depend on:

  • Purity (karat), weight and current spot price
  • Craftsmanship and finish
  • Presence & quality of gemstones
  • Brand reputation, after-sales service and warranty

Buying tip: Obtain a full invoice with weight, purity (karat), making charges and tax breakdown. Compare making charges across stores; they vary by design complexity and brand.

Care & maintenance

  • Clean gently with warm water and mild detergent, soft brush; rinse and dry thoroughly.
  • Avoid chlorine and harsh chemicals — they can damage alloys and settings.
  • Store separately in pouches to avoid scratching (gold can scratch other softer metals/gems).
  • Inspect prongs and mountings regularly; tighten or re-plate white/rhodium finishes when worn.
  • Insure high-value pieces and keep receipts/certificates for claims.

Sustainability & ethical sourcing

Responsible jewellers prioritise:

  • Supply chain transparency — traceability back to mines or refiners
  • Using recycled gold and responsible refiners
  • Audited suppliers and fair-labour practices
  • Third-party certifications (Responsible Jewellery Council, Fairmined, Fairtrade Gold)
Questions to ask sellers

Do you offer recycled gold? Can you provide supplier/refiner details? Do you support any responsible sourcing certifications?

Metallurgy: forming, annealing & hardness

Gold alloys are processed by heating (annealing), cold working (hammering/drawing) and heat treating to achieve the desired balance of ductility and hardness. Key points:

  • Annealing: Softens metal after work-hardened operations so it can be formed without cracking.
  • Work hardening: Increases strength via hammering or rolling; excessive work hardening requires annealing.
  • Soldering: Special solders (matching karat/color) join parts; proper soldering is essential for durability.

Regional styles & availability

Different regions favour different karats and designs:

  • India & South Asia: 22K and 24K traditional jewellery; heavy bridal sets and elaborate gold work.
  • Middle East: High demand for 22K–24K and large, ornate pieces often with intricate filigree.
  • Europe & North America: 14K–18K popular for daily-wear due to durability; modern minimal styles common.
  • East Asia: Mixture — traditional yellow gold jewellery remains popular alongside contemporary 18K designs.

Glossary & helpful terms

Karat
Measure of gold purity (24K = 100% gold).
Hallmark
Official stamp indicating purity and sometimes assay office.
Vermeil
Thick gold plating over sterling silver; higher quality plating standard.
Rhodium plating
Thin plating used on white gold to create bright white finish.
Alloy
Metals mixed with gold (e.g., copper, silver, palladium) to change color and mechanical properties.
